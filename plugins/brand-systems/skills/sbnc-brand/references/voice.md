# SBNC Voice and Language

## Primary tagline

> **Campus-owned. SBNC-supported. Student-driven.**

Used as a banner element, in coral uppercase (Archivo Black, +1.4px tracking). Appears on the cover of partner-facing materials and at the top of internal docs.

## Mission line

> SBNC ensures students are identified, enrolled, and supported in public benefits and basic needs resources so financial insecurity does not prevent college completion.

Used as a single declarative paragraph on intro slides, "About SBNC" sections, funder materials, and the opening of any partner-facing toolkit.

## Three pillars

> **Technology · Campus Engagement · Data Insights**

The three things SBNC does, in this exact order. Each is one or two words. Use when introducing what SBNC offers.

Pillar specifics:
- **Technology**: Navvy deployment, Peer Navigators
- **Campus Engagement**: State-based Cohorts, Resource Hub
- **Data Insights**: Data Dashboard

## Partnership framing

> You bring context and relationships. SBNC provides tech, training, support, and data.

Used when speaking to campus partners about the working relationship. Reinforces that campuses lead, SBNC supports.

## Voice patterns

**Three-part parallel structure** is the dominant rhythm. Lock it in whenever you can:
- Campus-owned. SBNC-supported. Student-driven.
- Technology. Campus engagement. Data insights.
- Identified. Enrolled. Supported.
- Context. Relationships. Implementation.
- Capture attention. Build intention. Facilitate action.

**Direct, action-oriented sentences.** Start with a verb when possible:
- "Ensure students are identified."
- "Apply for SNAP/FoodShare."
- "Check if you qualify."

Avoid leading with hedges ("We believe that...", "It is the case that...").

**Specific, concrete framing over abstract framing.** SBNC's materials lead with concrete benefits and concrete numbers:
- "Up to about $300/month for groceries" — not "potential financial assistance"
- "About 10–15 minutes to check eligibility" — not "a quick eligibility assessment"
- "Many students at [Campus] qualify" — not "broad eligibility criteria"

## Do / Don't language

**Do say:**
- *students* (not "individuals," "consumers," "clients")
- *campus* / *campus partner* (not "school," "institution" — unless context demands)
- *basic needs* (not "welfare," "assistance," "aid programs")
- *check eligibility* (not "apply" — unless the link actually goes to an application form)
- *may qualify* (not "you qualify" — never claim eligibility before screening)
- *peer navigators* (not "volunteers," "ambassadors")
- *SNAP/FoodShare* in Wisconsin contexts; *SNAP* elsewhere

**Don't say:**
- *you qualify* (before screening)
- *claim your benefits*, *free money*, *unlock your benefits*
- *welfare*, *handout*
- *poor students*, *struggling students*, *at-risk students* — non-stigmatizing framing only
- *our records show…* — unless the campus or SBNC actually has that data
- *URGENT*, *FINAL*, *DEADLINE* — when no real deadline exists (no fake urgency)
- *vendor*, *platform*, *solution* — when describing Navvy to students (Navvy is a tool; students see it as the eligibility check)

## Tone by audience

**To students** — warm, direct, non-stigmatizing. Use "you" and address by first name. Lead with the concrete benefit (groceries, dollars per month, time estimate). Acknowledge that benefits eligibility is broader than people expect.

**To campus partners** — institutional, partnership-focused, capability-building. Frame SBNC as supporting, not directing. Use the "you bring / we provide" structure. Recognize the partner's expertise on their own campus.

**To funders / board** — outcomes-oriented, evidence-based. Lead with hard numbers (3,600 SNAP submissions target, students screened, retention impact). Cite sources for stats. Use the mission line as the anchor.

**Internal** — direct, plain, light on jargon. The same brand voice but tighter; can use abbreviations like "WBA" (Wisconsin Benefits Access cohort), "the cohort," team-internal nicknames.

## Stat callout pattern

When citing a research stat in a slide or one-pager:

1. Big white display headline on navy background.
2. **One operative word in coral** (the part that names the problem or outcome).
3. Small italic mid-gray citation in lower-right.

Example structure:
> 59% of students have considered dropping out due to **financial strain**
> *Ellucian & EMI Research Solutions, 2024*

The coral word should be the most "load-bearing" part of the sentence — the thing the reader is supposed to remember.

## Approved variants of the tagline

Use the full tagline when possible. When space is tight, these short forms are approved:

- *Campus-led. SBNC-supported.* (when the student-driven element doesn't add)
- *Powered by SBNC.* (for co-branded contexts where SBNC is the smaller credit)
- *In partnership with SBNC.* (formal report contexts)

Never abbreviate to just "SBNC" without context.

## Words that signal off-brand

If you find yourself reaching for any of these, pause and rewrite — they're not in SBNC's voice:

- *leverage*, *synergy*, *stakeholders*, *bandwidth*, *deliverable*
- *cutting-edge*, *innovative*, *transformative*, *revolutionary*
- *empower*, *uplift*, *amplify* (when used about students — sounds patronizing)
- *low-income students* (use "students who may qualify for benefits" or be specific)
- *underserved* / *vulnerable* (use specific, non-deficit framing)

## Punctuation patterns

- **Em dash** ( — ) is used heavily for asides and parenthetical phrasing. Surround with spaces: `text — aside — text`.
- **Center dot** ( · ) is used in three-part lists and pillar phrasing: `Technology · Campus Engagement · Data Insights`.
- **Period at end of each tagline element** — even when the elements are short: `Campus-owned. SBNC-supported. Student-driven.` (NOT `Campus-owned, SBNC-supported, student-driven`).
- **Right single quote** for apostrophes (`'` not `'`) — basic professional typography.
- **No Oxford comma in three-pillar lists** when separator is center dot; use Oxford comma in standard prose.
