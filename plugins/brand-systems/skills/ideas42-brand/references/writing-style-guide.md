## 1. Terminology & Identity

### Who We Are and Who We Work With

- **Partners**: Organizations we work with to design behaviorally informed solutions. If they're also paying us, they're still a partner (not a funder).
- **Funders**: An individual or organization paying for us to work on programs it does not itself run. If the funder also runs the program, they're a partner.
- **Clients/Users**: The individuals who use the programs and products we design. Use a more specific group characteristic when it fits the context (e.g., voters, business owners).
- **Team members**: Someone who works at ideas42. This phrasing reflects our value of being a more horizontal organization. Do NOT use: employees, workers, staff.
- **U.S./Global**: The distinction for where we work is the United States vs. elsewhere (e.g., U.S. Health and Global Health). Do NOT use: domestic/international, as these are relative terms that center the U.S.

## 3. General Framing Guidance

- Adhere to ideas42 style guide when working with funders and partners unless they explicitly ask for their own terminology. When in doubt, check with Communications.
- Define behavioral science principles upon first use. Avoid internal jargon when common language is available unless there is a clear and compelling reason.
- Avoid myth-busting framing that repeats false messages (e.g., "you may have heard X, but it's really about Y"). This can reinforce the myth.
- Adhere to guidelines from focus area experts where appropriate (e.g., people-first language from the economic justice team).
- Follow ideas42's DEI guidelines.

## 4. Inclusive Writing

### Core Principles

**Self-determination**: Ask people how they want to be referred to — especially racial designation or gender pronouns. Ask about "person-first" vs. "identity-first" language preferences. Take the lead from the people we work with. Don't assume we know what's best. Avoid paternalism.

**Specificity**: Avoid sweeping terms that can be inaccurate or perpetuate harmful narratives. Be specific about who you're talking about rather than falling back on inherited terminology. For example, instead of broad terms like "developing countries," specify the actual nation, population, or context.

**Plain language**: Behavioral science can help everyone, but it's not easy to understand. Avoid using specialized vocabulary without explanation. If you must use a term, define it. Don't assume readers know what we know.

**Show where agency resides**: Use active voice (e.g., instead of "hundreds of people were disenfranchised," say who did what). It's often the system rather than a person who is responsible for poor outcomes — frame accordingly.

**Flexibility**: Language evolves. Different people have different preferences. Consistency is good, but respect for the people we write about is better.

## 5. Terms to Avoid

### Capacity building
Jargony and unclear. It "overvalues the perspectives and acumen of the capacity 'builder.'" Instead, consider **professional development**.

### Defendant
Avoid when possible. Use more specific or person-first language appropriate to the context.

### Empower
- OK to use when it fits the mold of actually giving people power (e.g., meaningfully shaping program designs, hiring/promoting staff).
- Do NOT use disingenuously or condescendingly (e.g., just being nice to people is not "empowering" them).

### Irrational/irrationality; rational actor
Behavioral scientists use "rational" in a specialized sense that differs from common usage. These terms can cause misreadings, and calling someone irrational has a level of paternalism. Avoid.

### Latinx
- Consult the specific communities involved for their preference.
- While there is growing acceptance in the U.S., ideas42 defaults to other identifiers because awareness and acceptance of the term remain low.
- Many community members prefer to identify using national origin or other terms.

### "Other ___"
In surveys, offering an "other" option can be othering. Consider alternatives like "not listed" or an open-text field.

### The poor / poor people
Use person-first language: "people experiencing poverty" or be specific about the population.

### Vulnerable
Avoid. Be specific about the conditions, systems, or risks affecting people rather than labeling them as vulnerable.

### Welfare or food stamps
Avoid unless providing historical context. Use current program names (e.g., SNAP, TANF).

## 6. Spelling & Punctuation

### Commas
Use the **Oxford (serial) comma**.

### Abbreviations
Generally avoid. Abbreviations are easier to type but harder to read. Unless an abbreviation is so familiar it's used more often than the full form (e.g., AIDS, BBC, CIA, FBI, HIV, IMF, NATO, NGO, OECD, UNESCO), spell it out on first use and abbreviate after:
> "We worked with the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) to design and test … UNEP's goals are to …"

### Headings and Titles
Capitalize the first letter of every word except articles (the, an) and prepositions (to, by, for, etc.). Capitalize the first word after a colon, and the first and last word always.

### Nontitle Capitalization
Restrict to: beginning of sentences, widely accepted proper nouns, titles, team names, and honorifics. Do NOT capitalize:
- Names of theories (prospect theory)
- Behavioral concepts (mental accounting, status quo bias)
- Roles used generically (the director, not the Director)

### Seasons and Months
Prefer specifying the month rather than giving the season, because seasons vary by hemisphere and ideas42 is a global organization.

### Quotation Marks
- **Double** quotation marks for spoken word or text excerpts.
- **Single** quotation marks only for quotes within quotes.
> E.g., "When I say 'immediately,' I mean some time before April," said the spokesman.

### Hyphens and Dashes
- Do NOT hyphenate adverbs ending in -ly + adjective/participle (casually dressed person, NOT casually-dressed person).
- DO hyphenate compound modifiers before a noun (long-term plan, NOT long term plan), but the hyphen is optional if the noun comes before the modifier.
- **Em dashes** can replace commas for emphasis. Close them with the words (no spaces).
> Upon discovering the errors—all 124 of them—the publisher immediately recalled the books.
- A single em dash at end of sentence can replace parentheses.

### Spacing
Single space after periods.

### Format
Justify text on external-facing documents when the option is available.

### International Spellings
Use American English unless the partner/funder requires otherwise or it's part of their name.

### Charts and Tables
Should be understandable without reading accompanying text. Give all charts and tables a title and clearly label axes.

### Hyperlinks
Link text alone should convey the purpose. No generic "Read More" or "Click Here." Link short strings of text rather than full URLs or full sentences.

## 7. Numbers & Formatting

- Spell out numbers one through nine; use digits for 10 and above.
- Use numerals for thousands (100,000 not 100k or 100 thousand).
- Write out "million" and "billion" ($10 million, not $10m).
- Avoid superscripts except for references (7th grade, December 10, twentieth century).
- Write $0.10 rather than 10¢.
- Use % rather than "percent" or "per cent."
- Use numerals for any number with a decimal point (e.g., 4.25) even if less than 10.

## 8. References, Footnotes & Tables

### Statistics
- In project briefs or lay-audience materials, include p values and statistical tests as footnotes, not inline.
- Use "n =" only in graphs to save space; in text, describe the sample ("the text messages were sent to 526 people in the treatment group").

### Citations
Follow APSA guidelines for citing books, papers, and articles.

### Footnotes
- Use Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3), not Roman numerals or symbols.
- Multiple footnotes in one sentence should be distinct numerals.
- Footnotes at end of a sentence go outside the punctuation.

### Section Headings
Use plain Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3) for sections or lists. For nested lists, use 1.3, 2.4.1, etc.
